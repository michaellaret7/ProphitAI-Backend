## Introduction to Commodity Market Drivers

Commodity markets are influenced by a complex interplay of factors including **supply and demand fundamentals**, **weather and natural events**, **economic data and growth indicators**, **geopolitical risks**, **currency and financial market movements**, **policy and regulatory influences**, and **technological shifts**. Each of these factors plays a different role depending on the specific commodity in question, such as oil, metals, or agricultural products.

## Overview of Key Categories in Commodity Markets

### Supply and Demand Fundamentals
- **Production Levels**: Changes in output affect supply.
- **Global Consumption Trends**: Industrial activity and consumer behavior impact demand.
- **Inventory Levels**: Stockpile levels provide insight into supply/demand balances.

### Weather and Natural Events
- **Agricultural Commodities**: Weather affects crop yields.
- **Energy Markets**: Climate events impact oil, gas, and coal production.
- **Metals & Mining**: Natural disasters or labor strikes can halt production.

### Economic Data & Growth Indicators
- **GDP Growth**: Increases demand for industrial metals, energy, and agriculture.
- **Manufacturing & Industrial Production**: Signals increased raw material consumption.
- **Employment & Consumer Spending**: Affects fuel and agriculture demand.

### Geopolitical & Supply Chain Risks
- **OPEC+ Decisions**: Impact oil supply and prices.
- **Sanctions and Trade Restrictions**: Shift trade flows for various commodities.
- **Shipping & Logistics Disruptions**: Affect global commodity transport.

### Currency & Financial Market Movements
- **U.S. Dollar Strength**: Makes commodities more expensive for foreign buyers.
- **Interest Rates & Inflation**: Influence the attractiveness of non-yielding assets like gold.
- **Speculative Positioning**: Drives volatility in futures markets.

### Policy & Regulation
- **Environmental Regulations**: Impact fossil fuel demand.
- **Biofuel Mandates**: Affect agricultural commodity demand.
- **Government Reserves**: Manage strategic commodity reserves.

### Technological Shifts
- **EV and Battery Metals Demand**: Increases demand for lithium, cobalt, etc.
- **Green Energy Transition**: Shifts in energy consumption impact fossil fuel demand.
- **AI & Semiconductor Boom**: Drives demand for copper, silver, etc.

## Interaction Among Commodity Drivers

These factors interact in complex ways:
- **Weather Events and Supply**: Droughts can reduce agricultural output, increasing prices.
- **Currency Movements and Global Trade**: A strong U.S. dollar can make U.S.-produced commodities more expensive globally.
- **Geopolitical Risks and Policy**: Sanctions on a country can disrupt global supply chains.
- **Economic Indicators and Technological Shifts**: Economic growth drives demand for metals used in technologies like electric vehicles.

## Example Scenario: Impact of a Strong U.S. Dollar on Oil Prices

A scenario illustrating how a strong U.S. dollar affects oil prices involves considering how currency valuation influences global commodity prices. When the dollar is strong, oil (priced in dollars) becomes more expensive for foreign buyers. This can lead to reduced demand from non-U.S. countries, potentially decreasing oil prices unless offset by other factors like stronger global economic growth or geopolitical tensions that restrict supply.

## Forward-Looking Perspective

Looking ahead, several factors are likely to influence the near-term commodity outlook significantly:

- **Inflation and Economic Growth**: Continued moderate inflation in major economies (e.g., around 3%) could support commodity prices by maintaining demand for raw materials[1][4].
- **Geopolitical Risks**: Ongoing or heightened tensions between major powers, especially those directly impacting key commodities like oil or grains, could drive market volatility[2][3].
- **Technological Shifts**: The increasingly rapid adoption of electric vehicles will continue to drive demand for specific metals like lithium and cobalt, shaping their price dynamics[1][4].

These factors highlight the complex and dynamic nature of commodity markets, where multiple drivers interact to shape market outcomes.

## Detailed Overview of Key Drivers Influencing ETF Performance

### Underlying Assets
ETFs derive their performance primarily from the assets they track. Equity ETFs are influenced by corporate earnings, sector growth, and stock valuations. For example, technology ETFs like XLK benefit significantly from innovations such as AI advancements, which drive corporate profitability and investor enthusiasm. Fixed income ETFs, such as TLT (tracking long-term U.S. Treasury bonds), are sensitive to interest rate changes—rate hikes generally reduce bond prices, leading to declines in bond ETF values. Commodity ETFs like GLD (gold) gain during times of economic uncertainty as investors seek safe havens. Currency ETFs, including UUP (tracking the U.S. dollar), reflect exchange rate fluctuations driven by central bank policies and global trade dynamics[3].

### Market Trends
ETF performance is also shaped by broader market conditions. Bull markets, characterized by rising asset prices, typically lift equity ETFs, while bear markets depress them. Sector rotations—such as shifts from growth-oriented technology to defensive utilities—impact sector-specific ETFs, affecting returns based on investor risk appetite. For instance, during risk-on environments, growth-focused ETFs like ARKK tend to outperform. Conversely, when investors seek safety, bond ETFs or dividend-heavy equity ETFs may gain favor[3][4].

### Economic Indicators
Key macroeconomic metrics drive ETF returns in different ways. Strong GDP growth generally boosts equities by supporting corporate earnings. Inflation erodes fixed income returns since rising prices diminish bond purchasing power and often lead to rate hikes that decrease bond valuations (e.g., adverse for BND). Interest rate hikes from central banks pressure bond ETFs and can create volatility in equity ETFs by increasing borrowing costs. Employment data signaling robust labor markets may heighten consumer confidence, benefiting consumer discretionary ETFs[3].

### Geopolitical Events
Geopolitical risks such as conflicts, trade tensions, or elections introduce uncertainty affecting ETFs globally. For example, the Russia-Ukraine war caused oil ETFs like USO to surge due to supply concerns. U.S.-China trade tensions pressure emerging market ETFs like EEM by increasing uncertainty and disrupting supply chains. Political transitions can generate volatility and impact broad market ETFs such as SPY, reflecting shifts in policy expectations[3].

### Regulatory Changes
ETF performance can be influenced by evolving regulatory landscapes. Financial oversight laws, such as SEC restrictions on leveraged ETFs, affect the availability and risk profiles of certain ETF categories. Tax reforms impacting capital gains or dividend taxation may sway investor preferences between income-focused and growth ETFs. Sector-specific regulations, for instance environmental policies and carbon taxes, benefit clean energy ETFs like ICLN by incentivizing sustainable investments and penalizing traditional energy[3].

### Technological Advancements
Technological innovation is a significant driver, especially for equity and thematic ETFs. Breakthroughs in AI fuel technology ETFs (e.g., XLK), biotech innovations boost healthcare ETFs (e.g., XBI), and progress in renewable energy supports ETFs focused on solar or clean tech such as TAN or QCLN. These advancements create new growth opportunities and attract investor capital, increasing performance potential in these sectors[3].

### Currency Movements
Exchange rate volatility impacts international and currency ETFs. A strengthening U.S. dollar tends to reduce returns for U.S.-based investors holding foreign ETFs (e.g., EFA), as foreign profits convert to fewer dollars. Currency ETFs like FXY (Japanese yen tracker) move with central bank policy and global risk sentiment, affecting hedged or unhedged global ETF returns[3].

### Fund-Specific Factors
ETF structural attributes also drive performance outcomes. Lower expense ratios improve net returns, as exemplified by broadly diversified low-cost funds like VTI versus higher-cost thematic ETFs. Liquidity affects trading costs and ease of entry/exit. Tracking error represents how closely an ETF follows its index; smaller errors mean more predictable exposure. Dividend yield distributions affect income-focused ETFs (e.g., DIV) and can make them more attractive during low-rate environments[3][4].

The various drivers interact complexly, often amplifying or offsetting ETF effects:

- **Inflation and Monetary Policy**: Rising inflation coupled with central bank rate hikes depresses bond ETFs like AGG due to higher yields pushing prices down, while simultaneously boosting commodity ETFs like DBC as commodities serve as inflation hedges. This dynamic incentivizes portfolio shifts from fixed income to real assets.
  
- **Geopolitical Crisis and Currency Strength**: For example, Middle East unrest elevates oil prices, benefiting USO. If accompanied by a strong U.S. dollar, emerging market ETFs such as EEM may suffer due to capital outflows and currency depreciation in emerging economies. This creates a divergence where commodity and safe-haven assets rise, while emerging markets lag.

- **Technological Innovation & Market Sentiment**: Breakthroughs in AI can heighten investor risk appetite, driving growth-oriented tech ETFs higher, while risk-averse flows may retract from these same equities. Simultaneously, regulatory support for clean energy can further reinforce thematic ETFs like QCLN, illustrating how tech advancements and policy shifts act in concert.

- **Regulatory and Tax Policy**: Increased environmental regulation (carbon taxes) can enhance clean energy ETFs and pressure traditional energy funds, while tax changes that favor dividends may boost high-yield equity ETFs.

### 1. Recent Economic Data and Government Bond Performance

**Inflation and Growth:**  
Recent forecasts indicate that inflation, measured by core PCE, is moderating in 2025, expected to ease from around 2.7% in early 2025 to approximately 2.2% by the end of the year and into 2026[4]. This decline in inflation pressure typically reduces the upward pressure on Treasury yields because investors require less inflation compensation. **Employment and Wages:**  
Employment data suggest a slightly rising unemployment rate, expected to peak near 4.1% mid-year before settling back to 4.0% by year-end[4]. This signals a modest cooling of labor market tightness, which reduces the risk of wage-driven inflation. Stronger employment growth historically correlates with expectations of Fed tightening, pushing yields up; conversely, moderation in employment can ease rate hike expectations and pressure yields lower.

**Impact on Treasury Market:**  
Given these data points, Treasury yields across maturities are influenced by a balance between signs of economic resilience and easing inflationary pressures. The 10-year Treasury yield was around 4.33% as of March 2025[5], reflecting a normalization from pandemic-era lows. The Federal Reserve’s patient approach to rate cuts (projected 50 basis points in 2025) also signals a cautious easing cycle, which likely supports stable to slightly lower yields in the short term[4].

---

### 2. Behavior of the 2s10s Yield Curve

**Historical Tendencies:**  
The 2s10s curve, which measures the spread between 2-year and 10-year Treasury yields, historically inverts when the Federal Reserve tightens aggressively or when growth outlook dims, signaling recession risk. An inverted 2s10s curve often precedes economic slowdowns, as short-term rates rise on Fed hikes while longer-term yields fall amid growth/inflation uncertainty.

**Current Environment:**  
As of early 2025, the 2s10s curve is likely showing relatively flat or mildly steepening behavior. The Fed’s patient stance with modest rate cuts contrasts with prior cycles of aggressive tightening. The flattening of the curve occurred earlier during peak rate hikes, driven by elevated short-term yields reflecting Fed policy. However, with inflation easing and growth steadying, the long-end yields (10-year) remain elevated but not excessively so, leading to a yield curve that has not sharply inverted but remains under pressure[4][5].

---

### 3. Underlying Drivers: Linking Data to Yield Curve Behavior

- **Federal Reserve Policy:**  
  The Fed’s rate decisions and forward guidance are the primary drivers of short-term yields, especially the 2-year note. The ongoing transition to a more patient rate-cutting cycle with only gradual reductions in Fed funds rates has led to short-term yields stabilizing near current levels rather than plunging[4].

- **Inflation and Economic Growth:**  
  Moderating inflation and steady growth support the long end of the curve, preventing a deep inversion. Investors demand compensation for inflation risk and growth prospects at the 10-year maturity, which keeps yields around 4.3%[5].

- **Geopolitical Risks and Flight to Quality:**  
  Heightened geopolitical tensions or equity market volatility would typically trigger a flight-to-quality, boosting demand for Treasuries and driving yields lower—especially on the long end. Equity sell-offs increase Treasury bids as a safe haven, often flattening or steepening the curve depending on which maturities see more demand.

- **Supply Dynamics and Fiscal Policy:**  
  Larger deficits and Treasury issuance can exert upward pressure on yields due to increased supply. However, if demand from foreign central banks or domestic mutual funds remains robust, this effect is cushioned.

---

### 4. Upcoming Factors Impacting Treasury Rates

- **Tariffs:**  
  Renewed or increased tariffs could slow global growth and raise inflation via supply chain constraints. This uncertainty might induce a flight-to-quality rally in Treasuries, pushing yields down, particularly at the long end.

- **Inflation Data:**  
  Continued moderation in inflation will likely temper upward pressure on yields. Conversely, any surprise acceleration in core inflation metrics could prompt the Fed to reconsider the pace of rate cuts, pushing yields—and potentially the short end of the curve—higher.

- **Flight to Quality (Equity Sell-Off):**  
  A significant equity market correction could lead to a surge in demand for Treasuries, lowering yields across the curve, with greater impact on longer maturities, which are more sensitive to risk sentiment.

- **Bond Issuance:**  
  Increased Treasury issuance due to fiscal deficits could pressure yields upward unless balanced by strong demand from domestic and foreign investors.

- **Passive Flows:**  
  Central bank actions, mutual fund purchases, and portfolio rebalancing inherently affect liquidity and demand. For example, if central banks reduce Treasury holdings, yields may rise. Conversely, inflows from mutual funds can support prices and lower yields.

- **CTA/Technical Drivers:**  
  Commodity Trading Advisors (CTAs) and technical trading strategies often amplify short-term Treasury yield moves. Positioning, momentum, and liquidity conditions can cause volatile yield swings independent of fundamentals.

---

### 5. Forward-Looking Perspective

Looking ahead, Treasury yields and the 2s10s curve will be influenced by a nuanced interplay of the above factors:

- The **Fed’s cautious rate-cutting path** likely prevents a steepening of the curve unless growth deteriorates faster than expected.
- **Inflation data near 2.2% core PCE** should cap extreme yield increases but keep yields above pandemic lows.
- **Geopolitical risks and equity market volatility** remain key wildcards that could cause temporary flight-to-quality rallies, flattening the curve.
- **Supply dynamics and passive flows** will need close monitoring, as changes in Treasury issuance or central bank behavior could shift yields notably.
- **Technical and CTA-driven volatility** may produce near-term yield fluctuations, especially if market liquidity tightens.

In sum, the Treasury market in 2025 is expected to reflect moderate economic growth with controlled inflation, a patient Fed, and episodic geopolitical and financial risks, leading to a relatively stable but cautious yield curve environment with limited inversion risk compared to prior cycles.

## Analysis of FX Valuation Methods and Key U.S. Dollar Drivers as of April 2025

---

### 1. Overview of Main FX Valuation Methodologies

**Parity Conditions-Based Valuation**

- **Purchasing Power Parity (PPP)**: Based on the law of one price; exchange rates adjust so that a basket of goods has the same cost across countries when converted. Serves as a **long-term valuation tool** for identifying currency misalignments.
  
- **Interest Rate Parity (IRP)**: Relates exchange rate expectations to interest rate differentials between countries, preventing arbitrage opportunities. Useful in explaining forward premiums or discounts in FX markets.

- **Real Exchange Rate (RER) Approach**: Adjusts nominal rates for inflation differentials to assess currency overvaluation or undervaluation relative to trading partners.

- **Monetary Model**: Focuses on relative money supply growth, inflation, and economic output to explain exchange rate movements over the long term.

**Fundamental Analysis-Based Valuation**

- **Balance of Payments (BoP) Model**: Examines trade balances, capital flows, and foreign reserves; persistent trade surpluses typically lead to currency appreciation, deficits to depreciation.

- **Asset Market Approach**: Considers relative returns in bond and equity markets; capital inflows support currency strength.

- **Behavioral Equilibrium Exchange Rate (BEER)**: Uses econometric models incorporating productivity, terms of trade, and net foreign assets to assess currency valuation in the medium term.

- **Debt Sustainability Approach**: Evaluates external debt levels; higher unsustainable debt tends to pressure currencies downward.

**Market-Based (Technical & Sentiment) Valuation**

- **Real Effective Exchange Rate (REER)**: A trade-weighted, inflation-adjusted index measuring relative currency strength.

- **FX Forward and Options Market Pricing**: Forward rates embed market expectations of future FX moves; options pricing reflects anticipated volatility and directional bias.

- **Technical Analysis**: Uses price charts and momentum indicators (e.g., moving averages, Bollinger bands) for short-term forecasts.

- **Market Sentiment Indicators**: Includes speculative positioning (CFTC Commitments of Traders) and carry trade dynamics reflecting risk appetite.

**Valuation Model Suitability by Time Horizon**

| Time Horizon    | Preferred Models                              |
|----------------|----------------------------------------------|
| Long-term      | PPP, Balance of Payments, Monetary Model     |
| Medium-term    | BEER, REER, Asset Market Approach            |
| Short-term     | Technical, Sentiment, Market Positioning     |

---

### 2. Key Drivers Influencing the U.S. Dollar

**Federal Reserve Policy & Interest Rates**

- The Fed Funds rate and forward guidance strongly influence USD demand as higher interest rates attract yield-seeking capital.
- FOMC meetings, dot plots, and Fed speeches shape market expectations on rate trajectories.
- Quantitative tightening (QT) reduces liquidity, generally supporting USD appreciation.
- Key Fed data include inflation reports (CPI, PCE), employment statistics (Non-Farm Payrolls, Unemployment), and GDP releases.

**Inflation & Economic Data**

- Rising inflation prompts Fed tightening, strengthening the USD.
- Strong labor market data support higher rates and USD appreciation.
- Robust GDP growth and consumer spending underpin USD demand due to capital inflows.

**Global Interest Rate Differentials**

- The USD benefits when U.S. rates exceed those of other major economies (ECB, BoJ, BoE).
- Divergent monetary policies, especially a hawkish Fed versus dovish counterparts, favor the USD.

**Geopolitical and Risk Sentiment**

- The USD acts as a safe haven amid global uncertainty—conflicts like Russia-Ukraine, Middle East tensions, or China-Taiwan risks boost USD flows.
- Trade disputes and sanctions (e.g., U.S.-China) affect trade flows and dollar demand.
- U.S. fiscal issues, including debt ceiling debates, can impact USD stability.

**Global Liquidity and Financial Market Conditions**

- Elevated equity volatility (VIX spikes) and banking system stress increase USD demand.
- Dollar funding shortages intensify need for USD liquidity.
- Commodity price movements influence inflation expectations and, indirectly, USD valuation.

**Trade and Current Account Balances**

- Persistent U.S. trade deficits tend to weaken the USD.
- Adjustments in foreign central bank USD reserves can affect demand.

## Introduction to U.S. High-Yield Bonds and Emerging Markets

High-yield (HY) bonds in both the U.S. and emerging markets (EM) are influenced by a range of factors including credit spreads, default risks, liquidity conditions, and macroeconomic influences. Here, we compare these factors between U.S. and EM high-yield debt markets.

## Primary Risk Factors and Market Conditions in U.S. High-Yield Bonds

The U.S. high-yield bond market is characterized by several key drivers:

1. **Credit Spreads & Risk Premiums**: Wider spreads indicate higher credit risk and market stress.
2. **Federal Reserve Policy & Interest Rates**: Higher rates increase borrowing costs, affecting refinancing and liquidity.
3. **Corporate Fundamentals & Default Risk**: Leverage ratios and earnings growth are crucial for debt repayment ability.
4. **Economic Growth & Recession Risk**: Slowdowns hurt lower-rated firms first.
5. **Market Liquidity & Fund Flows**: Inflows support the market, while outflows indicate risk-off sentiment.
6. **Geopolitical & Market Risk Sentiment**: Investors flee riskier assets during risk-off events.
7. **Sector-Specific Risks**: Vulnerabilities vary across sectors like energy, retail, and technology.

## Differences and Similarities in Emerging Markets High-Yield Debt

### Similarities:
- **Credit Spreads & Default Risks**: Like the U.S., EM HY bonds are sensitive to credit spreads and default risks. However, sovereign risk and currency fluctuations add layers of complexity in EM.
- **Economic Growth & Global Influences**: EM economies are often more volatile and sensitive to global economic trends.

### Differences:
- **Sovereign Risk & Political Instability**: These factors significantly impact EM HY bonds due to country-specific risks.
- **Currency Fluctuations**: Non-USD denominated bonds in EM are exposed to currency risks.
- **Liquidity Conditions**: EM markets often face liquidity challenges due to less developed financial systems.
- **Regulatory Environment**: Varied regulatory frameworks across EM countries can affect market dynamics.

### Unique Considerations in Emerging Markets:
- **Country-Specific Factors**: Political stability, local economic conditions, and government policies play a significant role.
- **Regional vs. Global Risks**: EM bonds can be influenced by regional economic conditions or global macro trends, such as commodity prices.
- **Investor Perceptions**: Foreign investors may have different risk perceptions for EM bonds compared to U.S. HY bonds.

## Additional Considerations Unique to Emerging Markets

In addition to the factors influencing U.S. high-yield bonds, emerging markets face unique challenges:

- **Sovereign and Country Risks**: These are critical in EM, affecting both government and corporate debt.
- **Currency Exchange Risks**: Since many EM bonds are not denominated in the local currency or are pegged to major currencies, currency fluctuations can significantly impact returns and risk.
- **Regulatory and Legal Frameworks**: Differences in legal systems and regulatory environments can complicate investor protections and recovery processes.
- **Infrastructure and Market Development**: Less developed financial markets in EM often result in lower liquidity compared to the U.S.

## Outlook for Emerging Markets High-Yield Bonds

The outlook for EM high-yield bonds in 2025 will be shaped by both global economic trends and local factors:

- **Global Influences**: Economic conditions in major economies, such as the U.S. and China, will influence EM markets through trade and capital flows.
- **Monetary Policy Alignments**: Central banks in EM countries may adopt policies that either align with or diverge from major economies like the U.S., affecting local interest rates and liquidity.
- **Local Economic Conditions**: EM countries will see diverse performances based on commodity prices, political stability, and domestic economic policies.
- **Investor Appetite**: The attractiveness of EM HY bonds will depend on global investors' risk appetite, driven by factors such as yield differentials with developed markets and perceived risk of EM investments.

In summary, while U.S. high-yield bond drivers provide a foundation for understanding EM high-yield debt, the latter is heavily influenced by additional unique factors such as sovereign risk and currency fluctuations. The outlook for EM HY markets in 2025 will depend on a delicate balance between these local and global forces.

## 2025 Global FX Outlook: USD, EUR, CNY, JPY  
### Overview of Macroeconomic Environment  
Global economic growth in 2025 is expected to be subdued and potentially recessionary, with growth forecasts ranging from 2.3% (UNCTAD) to around 3.3% (IMF), generally below the pre-pandemic trend of about 3.7%[1][5]. Heightened policy uncertainty and financial market volatility are key downside risks, with indicators such as the Economic Policy Uncertainty Index and US stock market volatility (“fear index”) reaching multi-decade highs in early 2025[5]. This environment amplifies risks to currency markets through risk aversion, capital flow volatility, and shifting rate differentials.

---

### Rate Differentials and Monetary Policy Outlook

- **USD (US Dollar):**  
  The Federal Reserve has maintained a relatively restrictive stance to combat inflation, supporting higher US interest rates compared to other major economies. Nonetheless, with signs of slowing US growth and financial market volatility rising, the Fed may pause or pivot cautiously in H2 2025. The sustained positive rate differential vs. EUR and JPY supports USD strength but may moderate if growth concerns escalate[1][2][5].

- **EUR (Euro):**  
  The European Central Bank (ECB) has also pursued tightening but is facing weaker growth prospects in the eurozone, compounded by energy and geopolitical risks that dampen inflation pressures. Lower growth and elevated policy uncertainty may restrain further rate hikes compared to the US, potentially causing a narrowing or even inversion of EUR/USD rate differentials over 2025[2][4].

- **CNY (Chinese Yuan):**  
  China’s economy is projected to grow slower amid structural adjustments and global demand weakness, but the People's Bank of China (PBOC) is expected to maintain a relatively accommodative monetary stance to support growth. Lower benchmark rates and capital controls limit the yuan’s yield appeal versus USD and EUR. However, geopolitical and trade developments alongside China’s gradual financial liberalization will influence capital flows and CNY stability[1][3].

- **JPY (Japanese Yen):**  
  The Bank of Japan (BoJ) has kept ultra-loose policy with deeply negative rates, diverging from tightening trends elsewhere. This large rate differential has historically pressured the yen lower, but risk-off episodes often trigger safe haven demand that can temporarily strengthen JPY. Continued low yields amid global volatility and modest growth forecast imply persistent JPY softness with episodic volatility spikes[2][5].

---

### Capital Flows and FX Market Implications

- **USD Flows:**  
  Elevated US rates and global uncertainty sustain the USD as a primary reserve and safe-haven currency, attracting capital inflows especially during risk-off shifts. However, the risk of US growth slowdown and policy shifts may introduce periods of USD retracement or volatility spikes[5].

- **EUR Flows:**  
  Europe faces the challenge of capital outflows amid weaker growth and political uncertainties, with investor preference possibly shifting towards USD or safe-haven assets. However, structurally stable eurozone institutions and trade ties help moderate extreme EUR depreciation risks[2][4].

- **CNY Flows:**  
  Capital controls and cautious foreign investor sentiment limit large-scale yuan capital inflows/outflows. Yet, China's strategic role in global trade and selective opening of capital markets may encourage stable or incremental inflows, contingent on geopolitical context[3].

- **JPY Flows:**  
  Yen capital flows are strongly influenced by risk sentiment. In stable or risk-on environments, outflows tend to dominate due to low yields. During financial turbulence, safe-haven inflows cause sharp but usually temporary JPY appreciation[5].

---

### Macro Risk Scenarios Impacting FX

- **Global Recessionary Risk:**  
  With growth potentially slowing below 2.5%, recession risks increase, intensifying safe-haven demand (supporting USD and JPY) and pressuring risk-sensitive currencies like EUR and CNY[5].

- **Financial Market Volatility:**  
  High volatility and policy uncertainty could cause sharp, sudden FX corrections and capital flight to USD and JPY, with EUR and CNY more vulnerable to depreciation[5].

- **Geopolitical and Trade Fragmentation:**  
  Growing geoeconomic fragmentation risks disrupting trade and capital flows, potentially fragmenting currency blocs and increasing FX volatility[5].
