# Analysis of U.S. Dollar Valuation Methods and Key Drivers as of May 2025  

## 1. **Primary FX Valuation Approaches**  
### a. **Parity Conditions-Based Valuation**  
- **Purchasing Power Parity (PPP):**  
  PPP compares price levels of identical goods across countries to determine long-term equilibrium exchange rates. For example, the Big Mac Index highlights currency misalignments. As of 2025, the USD remains overvalued against several emerging markets (e.g., China’s yuan) but aligns more closely with PPP-adjusted rates for developed economies.  
  - *Example:* China’s forex reserves rose to $3.28 trillion in April 2025, partially offsetting USD overvaluation pressures through currency management.  

- **Interest Rate Parity (IRP):**  
  IRP links exchange rates to interest rate differentials. The formula \( F_0 = S_0 \times \frac{1 + i_a}{1 + i_b} \) determines forward rates, ensuring no arbitrage. With the Fed maintaining rates at 4.25–4.50% in May 2025, the USD’s yield advantage over the ECB (2.4%) and BoJ (0.5%) supports its strength.  

- **Real Exchange Rate (RER):**  
  RER adjusts nominal rates for inflation differentials. The USD’s real effective exchange rate (REER) has stabilized near 2.8% above its 10-year average, reflecting persistent inflation (April 2025 CPI: 2.3%).

  The U.S. trade deficit widened to a record $140.5B in March 2025, driven by tariffs and import surges ahead of anticipated policy changes. This structural deficit pressures the USD in the medium term.  

- **Asset Market Approach:**

- **Behavioral Equilibrium Exchange Rate (BEER):**

- **Technical Analysis:**

## US Treasury Yield Curve Outlook: Q2/Q3 2025

**Overall Shape and Level**
- As of April 2025, the US yield curve is modestly upward sloping, with the 2-year Treasury yielding 3.86% and the 10-year at 4.34%[3]. Forecasts for the remainder of Q2 and into Q3 2025 expect the 10-year yield to remain in the 4.3%–4.4% range, with some variation depending on market volatility and macroeconomic news[1][3].
- Recent market turmoil in April 2025 saw long-end yields surge—particularly the 30-year, which briefly touched 5%—reflecting shifting investor sentiment around US fiscal policy, inflation, and "safe haven" dynamics[4].

**Yield Curve Dynamics**
- CC&L Investment Management and other institutional outlooks expect the yield curve to "steepen," meaning long-term yields will rise more than short-term ones[4]. This reflects both higher inflation risks and shifting perceptions about US fiscal stability.
- Drivers: Stubborn inflation, technical flows/liquidity squeezes, concerns about the dollar's role as a reserve currency, foreign investor outflows, and fiscal deficit worries all contribute to upward pressure on long-term rates[4].

## Consensus on Fed Policy Path

**Pace and Magnitude of Cuts**
- The consensus for Q2/Q3 2025 is for a cautious, gradual pace of rate cuts. The University of Michigan’s RSQE forecast expects the Fed to deliver only a total of 50 basis points (bps) of cuts for the full year 2025—projecting a deliberate, data-dependent approach given only slowly moderating inflation[5].
- By year-end 2025, the federal funds rate is projected to be in the 3.5%–3.75% range (from a current level closer to 4%–4.25%)[5].

**Inflation and Labor Market Context**
- Core PCE inflation is expected to fall to about 2.2% annualized by Q4 2025 (from 2.7% in early 2025), while the unemployment rate is forecasted to hover around 4%–4.1%[5].

## Implications for 7-10 Year Treasury and Investment Grade Corporate Bonds

**Treasury Return Outlook**
- If the Fed proceeds with 50bps of rate cuts amid a still-steepening curve, 7–10 year Treasuries will likely see yields remain near current levels or drift slightly lower by year-end 2025.
- Price Return Potential: Should yields fall even modestly (e.g., from ~4.34% towards 4.0%), holders of 7–10 year Treasury securities could see positive total returns, combining the yield (carry) with some price appreciation. However, if yields remain elevated or rise (as some fiscal risk scenarios suggest), returns will be limited primarily to the coupon.

**Investment Grade Corporate Bond Outlook**
- Yield Spreads: Investment grade corporate bonds are priced with spreads over Treasuries; these have remained relatively tight, given low default risk and stable corporate conditions in 2025.
- Return Prospects: If rate cuts are gradual and economic conditions don’t deteriorate, 7–10 year investment grade corporates could deliver returns in the 4.5%–5.5% range (current yields plus modest price gains if Treasury yields compress slightly or spreads narrow further).
- Downside Risks: If inflation surprises to the upside or safe haven concerns persist, upward pressure on Treasury rates could dampen returns for both Treasuries and corporates. Furthermore, any material widening of credit spreads (due to recession fears) would negatively impact the total returns of corporate bonds.

## Key Takeaways for Portfolio Construction

- The US Treasury yield curve is expected to remain modestly upward sloping, with the 10-year at ~4.3%–4.4% through Q3 2025[1][3].
- The Fed is likely to cut rates slowly—consensus expects only 50bps of easing for all of 2025[5].
- If these base case scenarios play out, the return outlook for 7–10 year Treasuries is mid-single digits, with corporate bonds offering a modest spread advantage.
- Risks remain tilted towards curve steepening (higher long-term rates), especially if fiscal concerns, foreign outflows, or inflation re-accelerate[4]. In such cases, overall bond returns would be more subdued.
- For investors, maintaining a diversified allocation across Treasuries and high-quality corporates in the intermediate-maturity sector (~7–10 years) balances yield, duration, and credit risk as the rate cycle turns.

## Spread Forecasts and Risk Premia

- Investment grade corporate bond spreads remain tight but are supported by strong demand. New issue supply is robust, and starting yields are at post-financial crisis highs, suggesting positive forward-looking total returns, even as spread compression may limit further price appreciation[5].

## Sector Risk Concentrations

- The highest default risks remain concentrated in sectors disproportionately hit by tariffs, supply chain disruptions, and cyclical headwinds. These include:
  - Retail and consumer discretionary

  - Industrial sectors sensitive to global trade policies[1][2][5]

## Key Takeaways

- IG corporate bond default risk stays very low through 2026; focus is on downgrade risk, not defaults[3][5].
- HY default rates are rising but are expected to remain below peak crisis levels; sector selection is crucial as risk is unevenly distributed[1][2][5].
- Spreads are tight but starting yields are attractive, offering a cushion against moderate volatility[5].
- Allocation strategies should favor quality, diversification, and active management to capture mispricings and limit downside as macro policy risks remain elevated[5].

In summary, the US corporate bond market faces a mild uptick in defaults largely confined to high yield and leveraged sectors, while investment grade remains resilient. - **US Economic Outperformance**: US GDP projected at 2.7% in 2025, outpacing most developed economies[1][5].

**Drivers of Weakness**
- **Extended Overvaluation**: The USD is seen as significantly overvalued based on historical metrics and elevated net-long positioning—the highest since 2015[5].

## 2025 Scenario Analysis: Tail Risks, Probability Estimates, and Hedging Strategies

### **Key Tail Risks for Global Markets in 2025**

**Geopolitical Risks**

- **Escalation of hybrid warfare**: Increased cyberattacks and sabotage on critical infrastructure, especially subsea cables and satellite networks in Europe, Asia, and the U.S. The scenario includes potential accidental strikes (e.g., Russian projectiles hitting NATO territory), escalating tensions without all-out war[5].
- **US-China confrontation**: The U.S. could intensify policies forcing global alignment against China, imposing tariffs and trade restrictions, potentially fracturing global trade and investment flows. An attempt to devalue the dollar through coordinated action (akin to a new 'Plaza Accord') could destabilize currency markets[5].
- **Emergence of new 'spheres of influence'**: A 'grand bargain' could confine powers geographically, leading to pronounced global economic fragmentation and selective decoupling, raising transaction costs and reducing cross-border investment[5].

**Elections and Political Uncertainty**

- Major elections in the U.S., European Union, and key emerging markets increase policy and regulatory uncertainty. Shifts towards more protectionist or populist policies could lead to sudden changes in trade, tax, or regulatory regimes, especially if disputed outcomes or social unrest arise[1][5].

**Policy Mistakes**

- **Monetary policy over-tightening**: Central banks, fearing inflation persistence or wage-price spirals, may overtighten, triggering recessions and sharp risk asset sell-offs[2].
- **Premature easing or fiscal slippage**: Looser policy in response to economic softness could reignite inflation expectations, destabilizing sovereign bond markets and currencies, particularly in vulnerable emerging markets[2].

**Liquidity Shocks**

- **Sudden liquidity crunch**: Disruptions in U.S. Treasury or European sovereign debt markets, driven by policy errors, geopolitics, or technical system failures, could spill into broader credit markets. The risk is amplified by market concentration and less robust market-making in the post-crisis environment[4].
- **Non-bank financial instability**: Stress in shadow banking or private credit could propagate rapidly, given the sector's growth and interconnectedness[4].

### **Recommended Hedging Strategies**

**Multi-Asset Diversification**

- Increase allocations to high-quality government bonds (e.g., U.S. Treasuries, Bunds) as liquidity buffers and risk-off hedges, especially with heightened recession or market shock risk[4].
- Gold and selected commodities as hedges against currency instability and geopolitical shocks; gold historically performs well during geopolitical crises[5].

**Equity and Credit Hedging**

- Tactical use of equity index puts or put spreads to hedge material market drawdown risk.
- Consider sector rotation towards defensive industries (healthcare, utilities, consumer staples) and away from global cyclicals or companies with high China/U.S. trade exposure[2].
- Monitor credit spreads; widen allocation to investment-grade over high-yield debt to protect against liquidity crunches[4].

**Currency and Geopolitical Hedging**

- Short duration in vulnerable emerging market currencies and exposure to safe-haven currencies (USD, CHF, JPY) to mitigate FX tail risks related to policy or geopolitical shocks[5].
- For portfolios with direct China risk, explore CNY hedges or proxy hedges through Asian FX or related ETFs.

**Liquidity Management**

- Maintain elevated cash balances and access to short-term funding facilities to respond to abrupt liquidity needs[4].
- Conduct regular stress tests on portfolio liquidity and counterparty exposures, especially to non-bank institutions.

**Optionality and Alternatives**

- Consider tail-risk hedging products (e.g., VIX futures/options, tail-risk funds) that explicitly profit from volatility spikes and correlations breakdowns during systemic events.
