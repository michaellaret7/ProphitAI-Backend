# The Deterrence Super-Cycle: Why U.S. Defense Stocks Are the Best Risk-Adjusted Trade in This Market

*ProphitAI | April 13, 2026*

---

The last time the United States proposed increasing its defense budget by 50% in a single year, Dwight Eisenhower was in office and the Soviet Union had just launched Sputnik. We are living through the second such moment.

President Trump's FY2027 budget request calls for **$1.01 trillion in defense spending** — a figure that would have seemed fantastical even 18 months ago. Add the NATO mandate for allies to spend 5% of GDP on defense and security, an active U.S.-Iran military campaign in the Persian Gulf, and contractor backlogs at all-time highs, and you get something rare in equity markets: a structural demand shock backed by sovereign spending commitments that are extraordinarily difficult to reverse.

This isn't a momentum chase. It's a thesis about locked-in earnings visibility, bipartisan political support, and a geopolitical regime change that reprices the entire defense sector for a decade. The market has started to figure this out — the SPDR S&P Aerospace & Defense ETF (XAR) is up 65% over the past year — but the catalyst calendar ahead is loaded, and the structural case is far from fully priced.

Here's the trade, why it works, and exactly how to manage it.

## The Setup: Three Pillars of a Spending Reset

### Pillar 1: The Budget Shock

The FY2027 defense budget proposal represents the most aggressive shift in U.S. military spending since the Reagan buildup. JPMorgan's fixed income research team flagged the scale of the proposal as early as January 2026, noting the trajectory implies a **10-year commitment of $13.5 trillion** in defense outlays [JPMorgan U.S. Fixed Income Weekly, January 2026].

This isn't aspirational — it's anchored to specific programs. The Golden Dome missile defense architecture alone is projected at $185 billion. Virginia-class submarine procurement is accelerating. The B-21 Raider stealth bomber is ramping toward full-rate production. And munitions replenishment from Operation Epic Fury demands billions in new orders that didn't exist six months ago.

Congressional opposition exists, but defense spending has a rare bipartisan constituency. Even if the final appropriation lands 15-20% below the request, the floor is dramatically higher than current levels.

### Pillar 2: The NATO Rearmament Mandate

In 2024, NATO allies struggled to meet the 2% GDP defense spending target. By early 2026, the goalpost moved to **5% of GDP** — 3.5% for core military spending plus 1.5% for broader security infrastructure [JPMorgan Japan Fixed Income Weekly, January 2026].

This isn't just a U.S. story. The EU has committed to **€850 billion in additional defense spending over four years**, with procurement heavily concentrated in integrated air and missile defense systems — exactly the product portfolio of U.S. prime contractors like RTX and Lockheed Martin. RTX's management explicitly noted this: their international defense backlog hit a record $75 billion in Q4 2025, with international orders now representing 47% of the Raytheon segment.

### Pillar 3: Combat-Proven Demand

Operation Epic Fury — the U.S./Israel military campaign against Iran launched on February 28, 2026 — transformed theoretical defense spending commitments into urgent procurement reality [JPMorgan "Crude Awakening" Research, March 1, 2026; UBS Global Multi-Asset Strategy].

Patriot interceptors were consumed at rates that exposed critical munitions shortfalls. Tomahawk missiles were fired in quantities that validated multi-year replenishment orders. The Strait of Hormuz disruption demonstrated that U.S. naval capacity needed expansion, not maintenance.

Even under the April 8 ceasefire framework, the spending implications are locked in. Wars consume inventory. Replenishment orders are multi-year and high-margin. And the demonstrated vulnerability of U.S. readiness ensures that no future Congress — regardless of party — will cut defense spending to pre-2025 levels.

## The Evidence: Backlogs Don't Lie

Academic theories and geopolitical narratives are useful, but contractor backlogs are where thesis meets reality. Here's what the numbers say:

**RTX Corporation** reported a full-year 2025 book-to-bill ratio of **1.56x**, producing a record backlog of **$268 billion** — up 23% year-over-year. CEO Chris Calio stated on the Q4 2025 earnings call: *"We ended 2025 with roughly $161 billion of commercial orders and $107 billion of defense awards."* Free cash flow inflected to $7.9 billion, up $3.4 billion year-over-year — a transformation that validates the premium multiple.

**Northrop Grumman** carries a **$96 billion backlog**, representing over 2x annual revenue coverage — the best ratio among major primes. CEO Kathy Warden explicitly guided to FY2027 acceleration: *"We believe growth will accelerate based on what signals we are receiving regarding the FY27 US budget... B-21 and FAXX programs are pure upside"* not yet incorporated into guidance.

**Huntington Ingalls**, America's sole nuclear shipbuilder, raised its medium-term revenue growth guidance from 4% to 6% and is targeting another **15% throughput improvement in 2026** after achieving the same in 2025. With Virginia-class Block 6, Columbia-class Bill 2, and CVN-82 long-lead materials all expected in contract this year, HII is arguably the most irreplaceable franchise in the defense industrial base.

These aren't forecasts. They are contracted commitments backed by sovereign credit.

## The Trade: How to Express It

After analyzing five individual defense stocks and three sector ETFs across performance, risk, valuation, and fundamental quality, the optimal trade expression is a **two-position structure** — simple enough to implement in fifteen minutes with a standard brokerage account.

**First, equal-weight structure captures the breadth of the spending surge.** When a $1.5 trillion budget proposal lifts all contractors simultaneously, equal-weighting systematically outperforms cap-weighting — which over-indexes to mega-caps already trading at premium valuations. The numbers confirm this: XAR delivered a **65.2% annualized return** over the past year versus ITA's 54.0%, with a Sharpe ratio of 1.93.

**Second, risk-adjusted returns are exceptional.** XAR's 1-year alpha versus the S&P 500 is **+33.1%**, with an information ratio of 1.43 — meaning it generates substantial excess return per unit of tracking error. The maximum drawdown of -17.3% during the March pullback recovered within 57 trading days.

**Third, liquidity is adequate.** At $34.5 million in average daily dollar volume, a $25,000-$50,000 position represents less than 0.2% of daily flow. Bid-ask spreads are tight given $4.6 billion in AUM.

## Risk Management: The Other Half of the Trade

A trade without a risk framework is a wish. Here's the complete structure.

### Position Sizing

**Recommended allocation: 5-8% of total portfolio.**

At 5%, this represents a meaningful conviction bet without portfolio-level concentration risk. The defense sector has low correlation to many traditional portfolio holdings (RTX's beta is 0.42; NOC's is 0.06), so the incremental risk to a diversified portfolio is modest relative to the return potential.

Split the allocation approximately:

- **65% in XAR** (core sector exposure)
- **35% in RTX** (satellite for alpha and earnings catalyst)

For a $300K portfolio, that's roughly $10,000-$12,000 in XAR and $5,500-$6,500 in RTX.

### Stop and Exit Criteria

**Reduce triggers** (cut position by 50%):

- XAR closes below its 200-day moving average (~$243) for three consecutive days
- RTX misses Q1 earnings AND cuts full-year guidance
- Iran peace treaty signed with comprehensive, permanent sanctions relief

**Full exit triggers**:

- FY2027 defense budget markup comes in below $1.0 trillion (signals Congressional rejection of the spending reset)
- Broad recession confirmed (two consecutive quarters of negative GDP growth) — defense holds up better than most sectors, but multiple compression in risk-off environments is indiscriminate
- XAR breaks below -25% from entry price

**Time-based reassessment**:

- **July 28, 2026**: Full review after Q2 earnings season and Iran situation update
- **October 1, 2026**: FY2027 actual budget number known — confirm or exit
- **January 15, 2027**: Nine-month mark. If thesis is intact, extend. If returns have compressed to single digits, reassess opportunity cost.

### Hedging Considerations

The biggest risk to this thesis is a comprehensive Iran-U.S. peace deal that removes the geopolitical urgency. A secondary risk is broad market multiple compression from an oil-driven stagflation scare.

**Natural hedges within the position structure:**

- RTX's 0.42 beta and 25% downside capture provide built-in defensive characteristics
- ITA (if chosen over XAR) has 0.88 beta and 85% downside capture — an even more defensive tilt

**Additional hedges for larger portfolios:**

- A small allocation to **GRID** (First Trust NASDAQ Clean Edge Smart Grid Infrastructure ETF) provides defense-adjacent exposure with different return drivers. GRID posted a 2.46 Sharpe ratio over the past year — the highest of any instrument analyzed — and had +5.2% 1-month momentum when defense ETFs pulled back -3.5%. The correlation benefit is real.
- Avoid pairing this trade with energy longs (XLE, XOP). Both defense and energy benefit from the same geopolitical risk premium — doubling up doubles the exposure to a single risk factor (Iran de-escalation).

## What Could Go Wrong

Intellectual honesty demands we stress-test the thesis. Three risks deserve direct attention.

**The ceasefire risk is real but manageable.** On April 8, defense stocks dipped when Iran peace talks were reported. This will happen again — every headline about diplomatic progress will produce a knee-jerk selloff. But here's the critical distinction: the ceasefire ends the *conflict premium* (perhaps 5-10% of current sector valuation), not the *structural spending reset*. The $1.5T budget proposal was made before Operation Epic Fury began. NATO's 5% GDP mandate predates the Iran war. Contractor backlogs of $268B don't evaporate with a peace treaty. The market may sell the headline; you should evaluate the substance.

**The "already run too far" objection.** XAR is up 65% in a year. Buying after a run this strong feels uncomfortable. But this objection confuses price level with valuation. RTX trades at 40x trailing earnings — elevated, but with $268B backlog, $7.9B FCF, and 10%+ forward EPS growth, the multiple is justified. NOC at 23x with a $96B backlog and B-21 upside optionality is arguably cheap. The sector isn't cheap in absolute terms, but the earnings power trajectory is still steepening. The FY2027 budget hasn't been priced because it hasn't been appropriated.

**Fiscal risk is the true tail risk.** A $1.5T defense budget in the context of $2T+ annual deficits raises real questions about fiscal sustainability. JPMorgan notes that the required Treasury issuance could increase term premium, steepening the yield curve and potentially compressing equity multiples broadly. The 30-year Treasury yield at 4.91% already reflects some of this concern. If the 10-year spikes above 4.75% on fiscal fears, the entire equity market — including defense — faces headwinds. This is the scenario where defense still outperforms (government demand is inelastic to rates), but the absolute return could disappoint.

The U.S. defense industrial base is entering a structural upcycle driven by three independently powerful forces — the largest defense budget proposal in modern history, a NATO rearmament mandate that extends the demand curve globally, and a live military conflict that has exposed critical capability gaps requiring years of procurement to fill.

**The trade:**

- **Buy XAR** (SPDR S&P Aerospace & Defense ETF) as a core position, scaling in over three tranches through early May
- **Buy RTX** as a satellite position ahead of April 21 earnings for tactical alpha with built-in defensive characteristics
- **Allocate 5-8% of portfolio**, split roughly 65/35 between XAR and RTX
- **Hold for 12-18 months**, targeting the catalyst arc from Q1 earnings through FY2027 budget appropriation

**Manage the risk:**

- Stop at the 200-day moving average for XAR; stop at -10% for RTX
- Reassess formally on July 28, October 1, and January 15
- Avoid doubling geopolitical exposure by not pairing with energy longs

**The expected return:** +12% to +20% in the base case, with asymmetric upside to +40% if the budget passes at scale and the spending cycle accelerates. Downside is bounded by defined stops and the structural floor under defense demand.

This is not a trade about war. It's a trade about the permanent repricing of national security as a budget priority — a repricing that has begun but is far from complete.
